Section Two

The Problem: Where GCC Companies Lose Time and Money

Every company setting up a GCC in India hits the same banking wall. It is not that they do not want to bank here. The problem is nobody is helping them get there fast, with the right partner, on the right terms, from the very first week.

  • Account opening takes too long. A foreign company incorporating an Indian entity and trying to open a corporate bank account without an introduction can wait three to eight weeks. In that window they cannot pay vendors, cannot onboard employees, and cannot run payroll. Every day of delay costs money.
  • FEMA and RBI rules are genuinely hard to navigate. Foreign currency accounts, export collection accounts, intercompany transfer structures, repatriation compliance: for a US or UK company setting up in India for the first time, this is unfamiliar territory. They need a bank that knows the rules and can walk them through it.
  • Banks come in too late. By the time a foreign company has incorporated and is shopping for a bank account, they are under time pressure and looking for the fastest option, not the best one. The right moment to be in the conversation is earlier: when they are choosing their city and building their cost model.
  • Cross-border banking is underserved. A GCC's parent company in New York or Dubai needs to transfer funds to the India entity, monitor balances, and manage treasury on both sides. Very few banks can genuinely serve both. SBI, with 229+ international offices across 30+ countries, is one of them.
  • Government scheme benefits get missed. STPI registration, DPIIT recognition, state IT incentives: all of these have banking implications. GCC companies miss these benefits not because they are ineligible, but because their bank does not have the government relationships to help them access the schemes. SBI does.

The fix is not a cold call after incorporation. It is being in the conversation before a company even picks a city. That is where the GCC Playbook sits. And that is where SBI needs to be.

Section Eight

Commercial Model: Hybrid Structure

The commercial model is designed to be low risk for SBI to enter and high value as the relationship grows. It has two components: a base annual partner fee and a per-account referral commission. SBI only pays for leads that actually convert.

Component 1: Annual Preferred Partner Fee

The annual fee covers exclusive co-brand positioning across the entire platform, SBI's name and contact in every blueprint output, lead routing infrastructure setup, a dedicated SBI landing page within the platform, and Pithonix's quarterly GCC lead market report shared with SBI's corporate banking team. This is the price of being the only bank GCC companies see when they are building their India plan.

Component 2: Per-Account Referral Commission

For every corporate account opened by a GCC company that came through a Pithonix platform lead, SBI pays a one-time referral commission. Tracked via a unique lead ID assigned at blueprint generation. SBI only pays when a lead becomes an active account. No payment for leads that do not convert.
